# Why Hindi Users Don't Make the 3rd Payment
## Problem Statement: Hindi A1/A2 3rd-Pay Retention

---

## The Number

**Hindi has the worst 3rd-payment rate of any major language.**

| Language | 3rd Pay % (of all paid) | 3rd Pay % (of 2nd-pay users) |
|---|---|---|
| Malayalam | 29.1% | 56.5% |
| Kannada | 26.2% | 51.4% |
| Marathi | 25.6% | 53.7% |
| Bengali | 24.5% | 52.5% |
| Telugu | 24.0% | 50.2% |
| Gujarati | 21.2% | 49.3% |
| **Hindi** | **16.2%** | **45.6%** |

Of every 100 Hindi users who pay once, only 50 pay a second time, and only 16 pay a third time. Among those 50 who did pay twice, 27 still drop off before the third payment. Hindi loses 54.4% of 2nd-payers before the 3rd payment, vs 43.5% for Malayalam.

This is a 11pp gap at the 2nd-to-3rd pay conversion step alone. Hindi isn't just acquiring fewer paying users -- it's losing them faster after they've already demonstrated willingness to pay.

---

## The 5 Problems Causing This

### Problem 1: The Curriculum Teaches Grammar, Not The User's Actual English Need

**What the data says:**

The top 100 most active Hindi A1/A2 "Work" users generated 218,750 messages. When we categorize what they practice vs what they actually need English for, there's a disconnect:

| What they practice (scenario content) | What they actually need (from message content) |
|---|---|
| "My friend was cooking dinner at 8 PM" | "Sir, fabrication welding progress is 70% on tank 3" |
| "She will be driving tomorrow" | "Dear sir, the material has been loaded and left Ahmedabad" |
| "Where is the hospital?" | "Please approve the newspaper leaflet activity amount" |
| "I like mangoes" | "The full and final payment for the scrap is 77000 rupees" |

25% of all user messages are in free-form mode (ai_speak_anything), not structured lessons. Within free-form, the #1 use is "Translate to English" (17,938 messages) -- users bringing their own workplace text for translation because no structured scenario exists for their actual need.

**What the qual says:**

8 out of 17 research call users (47%) -- the single most common pain point -- reported that app content doesn't map to their profession:

> *"Ma'am, jaise client ko hume convince karna hota hai, baat karni hoti hai. Ki makeup ke liye unhe batana padta hai..."* -- Saiba, freelance makeup artist, Trial Dropoff

> *"Writing skill chahiye, ma'am... confidently main ekdam baat kar sakoon bahut acche se, fluent hoke."* -- Divyani, civil services aspirant, Paid Dormant

Every Hindi user in the research calls had a completely different profession: makeup artist, physics teacher, CA student, civil services aspirant, retired gynecologist, tuition teacher, math researcher, school student. None got profession-specific content.

**Why this kills the 3rd payment:**

By the time a user reaches the 3rd payment decision (month 3+ for monthly, month 6+ for quarterly), they've completed 50-100+ activities. They've done the grammar drills. They've practiced "My sister and I were shopping at 2 PM yesterday" dozens of times. The question they ask at renewal is: **"Can I now do the thing I downloaded this app for?"**

For most Hindi Work users, the answer is no. The app taught them past continuous tense but didn't teach them to write a progress report to their boss or handle a client call. The curriculum built knowledge without building capability for their specific context.

**3rd-pay data confirms this:** The "need" categories with the highest 3rd-pay rates are ongoing daily needs -- "English doubts and translations" (30.2%), "grammar correction in emails" (26.3%), "improve spoken English" (26.1%). The lowest are event/context-specific needs -- "govt-exams" (5.5%), "abroad" (5.8%), "confidence" (6.9%), "interviews" (7.0%). Users with unmet specific needs churn; users with ongoing practice needs stay.

### Problem 2: Users Complete Activities But Can't Speak in Real Life (The Production Gap)

**What the data says:**

88.6% of user messages are under 50 characters. The dominant pattern is short responses to AI prompts: "Yes", "No", "I like mangoes", "He will be driving." Users are answering quiz questions, not producing real speech.

Even among the top 100 most active users (who averaged 2,187 messages each), the majority of their output is structured drill responses. Only 2.6% of messages are paragraph-length (>150 chars). The app is training input-processing (listen, understand, select correct answer) not output-production (think, construct, speak).

**What the qual says:**

6 out of 17 users (35%) reported this exact gap. The most striking case:

> *"main speak nahi kar paati, main connect nahi kar paati sentences ko"* -- Gungun, Power Loyalist, **126 activities completed**, ₹499 paid

Gungun has done 126 activities. She's classified as a Power Loyalist. By every engagement metric, she's a success. But she explicitly says she cannot form sentences in English. She would show up in dashboards as a retained, engaged user. In reality, she's a churn risk who hasn't experienced the outcome she's paying for.

The same pattern appears in Narmatha (124 activities, can complete all tasks but "struggle outside"), Savita (187 activities, "samajhne mein koi problem nahi hai... bas bolte samay thoda atak"), and Deb (354 activities, catches mistakes only after speaking -- "bol toh diye na?").

**Why this kills the 3rd payment:**

The 1st payment is hope. The 2nd payment is patience. The 3rd payment requires evidence. By month 3+, users have put in real effort. If they still freeze when their boss asks them a question in English, or still can't draft a WhatsApp message without help, the value proposition collapses.

The dangerous thing is that engagement metrics won't catch this. Activity completion rates look healthy. Streak counts look good. But the user's private assessment at renewal time is: "I've done 100+ lessons and I still can't speak." That's when they cancel.

---

### Problem 3: Hindi "Work" Users Are Primarily Monthly Subscribers, and Monthly Plans Mask a Deeper Retention Cliff

**What the data says:**

Monthly plans show a 60.5% 2nd-to-3rd pay conversion rate. Quarterly plans show 37.6%. Annual plans are near 0% (by definition -- most don't have a 3rd billing cycle yet).

This looks like monthly is "better" for retention. But it's misleading. Monthly plans have a 3rd payment at month 3. Quarterly plans have a 3rd payment at month 9. The quarterly drop to 37.6% means users who committed to 3 months TWICE (6 months total) still won't commit a third time. They've been using the app for half a year and 62.4% of them decide it's not worth continuing.

Hindi users are concentrated in lower pricing tiers (₹299/month) compared to Tamil/Malayalam users (₹3,500-5,000/year). The lower price point means:
- Lower switching cost = easier to cancel
- Less sunk-cost motivation to continue
- The app competes with "free" alternatives (YouTube, ChatGPT, Google Translate) at a lower price anchor

**Cohort degradation confirms the trend:** Earlier cohorts (May-Jul 2025) had 57-63% 2nd-to-3rd conversion. Recent cohorts (Nov 2025-Jan 2026) dropped to 27-36%. The product is getting WORSE at retaining payers over time, not better. This suggests either content saturation (early users got more novel content) or acquisition quality degradation (newer cohorts are lower-intent).

---

### Problem 4: The Onboarding Captures Profile Data But Never Uses It

**What the data says:**

The app asks users for their occupation during onboarding. The "Work" occupation category has 259,528 total paid users. But the curriculum they receive is identical to what a homemaker, student, or teacher gets. The occupation data goes into a database field and never influences the user's experience.

The 3rd-pay rates by occupation tell the story:

| Occupation | 3rd Pay % (of all paid) |
|---|---|
| Teacher | 31.6% |
| Home Maker | 29.6% |
| Other | 28.8% |
| **Work** | **24.1%** |
| School | 17.9% |
| College | 12.0% |

"Work" users (the segment we're analyzing) have below-average 3rd-pay rates. Teachers and homemakers retain better -- possibly because the generic curriculum (daily routines, family conversations, school scenarios) accidentally aligns better with their lives. "Work" users need workplace English but get household English.

**What the qual says:**

5 out of 17 research call users cited onboarding personalization failure:

> *"Nahi, woh toh yaad nahi hai. Woh select nahi kiya tha maine."* -- Arshad, possibly on wrong profile

> *"Agar mujhe koi aisa topic jo padhna hoga, toh main aapko message kar dungi. Toh fir aap usko kaise padhayenge?"* -- Divyani, no mechanism to request relevant topics

Narmatha (Tamil, software tester) explicitly provided her profession at onboarding and got nothing relevant back. The app asked and then ignored the answer.

**Why this kills the 3rd payment:**

The onboarding is a promise. When the app asks "What do you do?" the user expects the answer to matter. When lesson 1 is the same "I like mangoes" regardless of whether you said "construction supervisor" or "makeup artist," the promise is broken. The user gives the app a second chance (2nd payment). By the 3rd, they've concluded the promise will never be fulfilled.

---

### Problem 5: No Visible Progress = No Evidence to Justify Renewal

**What the qual says:**

4 out of 17 research call users mentioned lack of progress visibility:

> *"Ennoda progress eppadindradhu theriyala. But enakke theriyudhu. Chinna chinna improvement, chinna chinnadaa improvement maariye varudhu."* -- Suresh (Tamil, but the pattern applies to Hindi too)

The app has no "you were here, now you're here" dashboard. No "you've mastered these 4 tenses, 3 more to go." No before/after recording comparison. No proficiency score that moves.

**What the data says:**

Hindi 3rd-pay users who DO survive show 39.7 avg interaction logs and 7.7 active days (vs Malayalam's 42.4 logs and 9.3 days). Even the Hindi users who make it are less active than their Malayalam counterparts. This suggests that even committed Hindi users aren't finding enough reason to engage deeply.

**Why this kills the 3rd payment:**

Payment decisions are cost-benefit calculations. The cost is clear (₹299/month). The benefit is felt ("I feel more confident") but not measured. When a user's spouse asks "why are you paying for this English app?" -- the user has no concrete answer. No score improvement. No level-up. No certificate. No before/after evidence. The felt improvement gets rationalized away, and the payment stops.

---

## The Compounding Effect

These 5 problems don't operate independently. They compound:

1. **Onboarding captures "Work" but doesn't personalize** (Problem 4)
2. **So the user gets generic grammar drills** (Problem 1)
3. **They complete 100+ activities answering "Yes/No" to textbook sentences** (Problem 2)
4. **They still can't speak at work** (Problem 2)
5. **They have no dashboard showing progress** (Problem 5)
6. **At month 3, the monthly subscription auto-renews and they ask: "Why am I paying for this?"**
7. **They cancel.** (Problem 3 -- low price = low switching cost)

The Hindi user who DOES make it to the 3rd payment is the one who either:
- Has an ongoing daily need the app happens to serve (translation, email correction -- 3rd-pay rate 30%)
- Is intrinsically motivated enough to stick through generic content (teachers at 31.6%)
- Found the AI conversation feature and uses it as a judgment-free practice partner

Everyone else churns.

## What We Don't Know Yet (Research Gaps)

1. **No Hindi churned users were in the qual sample.** All 4 Hindi trial dropoffs were never-payers. Zero Hindi users who paid and then cancelled were interviewed. We're inferring churn reasons from active/engaged users' pain points, not from actual churners. Next research batch must include 5+ Hindi Churned Non-Renewers.

2. **We don't know the Hindi-specific competitive landscape impact.** Hindi has more English learning alternatives (Josh Talks, Duolingo Hindi, YouTube creators, ChatGPT) than Malayalam/Tamil. The 3rd-pay gap might partly be competition, not just product.

3. **We don't have cohort-level data on WHICH Hindi users churn at the 3rd payment.** Are they Work users? College users? Which need categories? The aggregate data shows Hindi is worst, but we need the Hindi-specific breakdown by need and occupation to prioritize fixes.

4. **We don't know if the cohort degradation (60%+ -> 27% 2nd-to-3rd conversion) is acquisition quality or product staleness.** If newer cohorts are lower-intent (from broader marketing), the fix is targeting. If it's content saturation, the fix is curriculum.

---

## The Fix (Summary)

The 5 problems map to 3 interventions, in priority order:

| Priority | Intervention | Problems Addressed | Expected 3rd-Pay Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **P0** | **Personalize the first 30 days by occupation.** Use the "Work" occupation signal + a follow-up question ("What do you do at work?") to serve workplace-relevant scenarios from day 1. Construction supervisors get report-writing practice. Teachers get presentation scenarios. Sales workers get client call practice. | Problems 1, 4 | +5-8pp on 2nd-to-3rd conversion for Work users |
| **P1** | **Build output-first speaking drills.** Replace 30% of "select the correct answer" MQ activities with "hear a Hindi prompt, speak the English sentence" production exercises. Track and show spoken fluency improvement over time. | Problems 2, 5 | +3-5pp by giving users evidence of speaking improvement at renewal decision point |
| **P2** | **Add a progress dashboard.** Before/after speaking recordings. Tenses mastered. Vocabulary count. "You spoke 40% more fluently this month." Surface this on the 25th day of each billing cycle -- right before the renewal decision. | Problem 5 | +2-3pp by giving users evidence to justify continued payment |

Combined estimated impact: closing 10-16pp of the 11pp gap between Hindi (45.6%) and Malayalam (56.5%) 2nd-to-3rd conversion rates.

*Based on: 478,055 conversation rows from top 100 Hindi A1/A2 Work users, cohort retention data (Jul 2025-Mar 2026), 3rd-payment data across 739K+ paid users, and 8 Hindi user research call transcripts.*
